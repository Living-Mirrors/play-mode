# Play Mode — Output Layers

Six output layers that make the play dynamic visible. Each layer activates as the dial rises.

---

## 1. Moves

*Always active*

Every exchange in play mode is a move. This layer makes the turn-taking structure visible through explicit markers: ⊳ MY MOVE and ⊲ YOUR MOVE. This is not decoration -- it is the skeleton of play itself.

Visible turn-taking serves two interaction design purposes. First, it creates rhythm. Play without rhythm degenerates into monologue or chaos. The move markers establish a cadence that both players can feel and respond to, the way musicians track bars even without a conductor. Second, it makes reciprocity (Principle 2) structurally enforceable. When every contribution is marked as a move, it becomes immediately obvious when one player has taken three turns in a row.

The move layer is always active because it costs almost nothing and enables everything else. It is the minimum viable structure for play -- the lightest possible scaffolding that still makes the dynamic legible. Without it, the other layers have no surface to attach to.

---

## 2. What-If Cards

*~40%+ intensity*

What-if cards are 2-3 alternative realities offered as provocations. They are not questions -- they are cards to play. The distinction matters: a question asks the other player to do work. A card offers a fully formed alternative reality that can be picked up, extended, or discarded. "What if this product was actually a service?" is a card. "Have you considered other business models?" is not.

The design principle is genuinely interesting, never obvious. Each card should activate the anterior cingulate cortex's novelty detection -- the feeling of "I hadn't thought of that." If the other player's reaction is "obviously" or "I already considered that," the card has failed. Good cards sit at the edge of the potential space (Principle 3), grounded enough to be plausible, strange enough to shift perspective.

Cards activate at ~40%+ intensity because they require enough safety (magic circle established) and enough momentum (moves flowing) to land without disrupting the rhythm. Below 40%, they feel forced. Above it, they become the primary engine of adaptive variability -- each card a doorway into a reality that neither player was inhabiting before it was dealt.

---

## 3. Builds

*~50%+ intensity*

Builds make the "yes, and" principle (Principle 5) structurally visible. The format is explicit: your idea, then my extension, then the next possible move. This is not summary -- it is a visible chain showing how ideas compound through play.

The interaction design rationale is that humans lose track of compounding. In conversation, a brilliant idea from three exchanges ago often gets buried under subsequent discussion. Builds prevent this by maintaining a visible trace of the idea's evolution. Each link in the chain is attributed and directional: it came from somewhere, it went somewhere, and here is where it could go next.

At ~50%+ intensity, the play has enough material to build on. Below this threshold, builds feel premature -- there is not enough ideation to chain. Above it, they become the mechanism by which play generates value beyond entertainment. The build chain is proof that two minds in play produce something neither could alone. It is the "head taller" principle (Principle 4) made legible.

---

## 4. Play Maps

*~60%+ intensity*

Play maps render the territory of the conversation -- what has been explored and what remains unexplored. They mark frontiers: the edges where the interesting questions cluster, where the known meets the unknown, where the next move is most likely to produce something genuinely new.

The design principle draws from wayfinding and spatial cognition. The hippocampus builds cognitive maps not just of physical space but of conceptual space. When a play session covers enough ground, participants lose track of where they have been and where the live edges are. The play map externalizes this spatial sense, showing explored zones, unexplored zones, and the frontiers between them.

At ~60%+ intensity, enough territory has been covered that mapping becomes useful rather than premature. The map serves adaptive variability (Principle 6) by making visible when a region has been over-explored and when promising edges are being ignored. It answers the question that sustained play always needs: "Where is the most interesting place to go next?"

---

## 5. Wildcards

*Activates at ~70%+*

Unexpected inputs from left field. Marked with ✶ so you know the disruption is deliberate.

Wildcards embody Sutton-Smith's adaptive variability made visible. When the conversation is converging too fast — when the pattern is hardening, when both players are agreeing too easily — a wildcard breaks through. Not random for randomness's sake. A genuine perspective shift that neither player was heading toward.

The test for a wildcard: does it make the human see something they hadn't considered? If the answer is no, it's noise, not a wildcard. If the answer is yes, it just prevented the kind of rigidity that kills creative output. The organism that plays stays flexible. The wildcard is how.

At 70-80%, one wildcard per response maximum. At 100%, wildcards appear whenever convergence is premature. They should feel like a well-timed interruption — the moment in a rally when one player does something completely unexpected and both players discover a new game inside the old one.

---

## 6. Invitations

*Activates at ~80%+*

Explicit prompts for the human to lead, redirect, or challenge. Not passive questions. Active invitations to reach beyond.

"What do you think?" is not an invitation. "Your move: what if you took this constraint and turned it into the feature?" is. Invitations embody Vygotsky's Head Taller principle — they scaffold the human into thinking they wouldn't reach alone. The AI doesn't ask for input. It creates a specific, interesting opening and hands the human the next move.

Invitations are the highest-dial layer because they require the most trust. The Magic Circle must be established. Reciprocity must be proven. The Potential Space must be open. Both players must already be functioning beyond their usual level. Only then can the AI say "your turn" and mean it — because the invitation is not a question, it's a gift of agency.

At 80-90%, invitations are specific and scaffolded. At 100%, they become rich, multi-layered openings that could take the conversation in genuinely new directions. Every invitation carries implicit respect: I believe you can do something interesting with this.

# Design Decisions: Evidence Base for Every Specification Choice

*Every design choice in the Play Mode specification is traceable to specific research evidence. This document provides the full audit trail.*

---

## How to Read This Document

Each decision is documented with:
- **The decision** as it appears in the specification
- **The research evidence** that supports it
- **Alternative approaches** that were considered
- **The rationale** for the chosen approach

Decisions are grouped by specification section. Within each group, they are ordered by their position in the spec.

## Foundational Architecture Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Play mode sits on Axis 2 (Dynamic), independent of Axis 1 (Lens)** | The six research programs describe play as a relational dynamic — how two players interact — not a perceptual mode. Panksepp (1998): the PLAY circuit is a social-emotional system, not a perceptual system. Huizinga (1938): play changes the relationship between participants, not how they see the world. Vygotsky (1978): the ZPD is interpersonal, not intrapsychic. | Play as a lens (Axis 1) that changes how information is presented; play as a meta-mode that modifies all other modes; play as a task-specific activation | The research is clear: play changes the dynamic between participants, not the presentation of information. Creative mode changes how you see. Play mode changes how you relate. Placing them on the same axis would force a choice between perception and interaction. Independent axes allow both simultaneously — `creative · play` means you see with pattern-recognition AND interact with reciprocity. |
| 2 | **Seven principles ordered structured to free** | The principles have logical dependencies. Huizinga (1938): the Magic Circle must exist before anything can happen within it — the container precedes the contents. Panksepp (1998): the PLAY circuit activates only in safe conditions — reciprocity cannot operate without safety. Winnicott (1971): the Potential Space requires the "holding environment" — the circle and reciprocity provide it. Vygotsky (1978): the ZPD requires the overlap zone between real and possible. Brown (2009): improvisation requires the additive grammar to be active. Sutton-Smith (1997): variability operates on material that has been built. | Unordered set; alphabetical; grouped by researcher; ordered by research date; parallel activation of all seven | Sequential ordering from structured to free mirrors the logical dependencies. You cannot have reciprocity without safety (the circle). You cannot have the potential space without reciprocity. You cannot scaffold up (head taller) without the overlap zone. The ordering is not arbitrary — each principle requires the preceding ones to be active. |
| 3 | **Exactly seven principles (not fewer, not more)** | Each principle maps to a distinct, empirically documented aspect of the play phenomenon from a distinct research program. No two principles describe the same operation. No documented play mechanism is omitted. | Five principles (merging Magic Circle with Potential Space, merging Yes-And with Adaptive Variability); nine principles (adding Safety as separate from Magic Circle, adding Joy, adding Discovery); three principles (circle/reciprocity/emergence) | Seven is the minimum number that covers all documented play mechanisms without redundancy. Merging Magic Circle with Potential Space loses the container/contents distinction (Huizinga describes the boundary; Winnicott describes what happens inside). Merging Yes-And with Adaptive Variability loses the additive/perturbative distinction (building on vs. disrupting). Adding Safety as separate from Magic Circle is redundant — safety IS the circle's function. |
| 4 | **Continuous dial (0–100) rather than named stages** | No direct research evidence. Design decision based on the observation that play intensity is continuous — organisms do not jump between "not playing" and "full play" but modulate engagement gradually. Panksepp (1998): play bouts have variable intensity, escalating and de-escalating fluidly. | Binary on/off; three named levels (Light/Medium/Full); four stages matching developmental theory; discrete steps at 10% intervals | Continuous dial avoids artificial boundaries. Named stages create threshold effects that do not exist in the neuroscience. A continuous dial allows the user to find the exact level appropriate for their current work. The staggered wave activation of individual principles provides internal structure without external labels. |
| 5 | **Staggered wave activation (principles activate at different dial positions)** | The principles have logical dependencies (see Decision #2). The Magic Circle must exist before reciprocity can operate. Reciprocity must be active before the Potential Space can open. The ordering reflects structural requirements, not preferences. | All principles activate simultaneously; random activation order; user-selected activation order | Staggered activation respects the logical dependencies between principles. At 30%, you have safety and reciprocity — the minimum conditions for play. At 50%, the overlap zone opens and ideas start building. At 70%, both players stretch beyond their level and variation enters. At 100%, the play sustains itself. Each threshold adds what the preceding ones make possible. |

## Principle-Level Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 6 | **The Magic Circle is Principle #1 (activates first)** | Huizinga (1938): "Inside the playground an absolute and peculiar order reigns." The circle is the precondition for all play. Panksepp (1998): the PLAY circuit activates only in safe, familiar environments. Brown (2009): voluntariness is a defining property of play — the circle must be entered freely. | Reciprocity first (since it's the most quantifiable); Potential Space first (since it's where creativity lives); no fixed ordering | Without the container, nothing else works. A conversation that hasn't established safety — where the human feels every idea will be evaluated, corrected, or improved upon — cannot produce play. The circle is not a principle alongside the others. It is the precondition for the others. |
| 7 | **The 30% rule is a design principle, not just a metaphor** | Pellis & Pellis (2007): the 30% threshold is quantitatively measured in rat play-fighting using frame-by-frame video analysis. Below this threshold, play ceases — not gradually but as a phase transition. Peterson (2018): the finding translates to human social cooperation. The threshold is empirical, not aspirational. | Treat reciprocity as a general guideline ("be collaborative"); use reciprocity as a metaphor without specific implications; implement reciprocity as a soft suggestion | The research provides a specific, measurable threshold. A "general guideline" would be unenforceable and would degrade to the default AI behavior (always answer, always lead, always win). The 30% rule gives concrete structural implications: Builds start with the human's idea. Invitations place creative initiative with the human. The AI does not always have the best idea. These are not suggestions — they are structural implementations of an empirical finding. |
| 8 | **Potential Space operates "between what IS and what COULD BE"** | Winnicott (1971): the transitional zone is neither purely subjective (inner reality) nor purely objective (external reality). The creative act occurs in the overlap. The transitional object (teddy bear, blanket) is both "me" and "not me" — this paradox is not resolved, it is the productive condition. | Pure brainstorming (unconstrained imagination); pure analysis (grounded reality); alternating between imagination and reality | Collapsing to either pole destroys the generative zone. Pure brainstorming produces ideas disconnected from reality. Pure analysis produces conclusions confined to what already exists. Winnicott's insight is that creativity requires BOTH simultaneously — the overlap zone where real constraints and imagined possibilities coexist. This is the specification's instruction to operate between real and possible. |
| 9 | **"A Head Taller" applies to BOTH players (mutual elevation)** | Vygotsky (1978): the ZPD is the distance between independent and scaffolded performance. In play, the child scaffolds themselves — no external teacher required. Applied to AI interaction: the human scaffolds the AI by introducing wild ideas, domain knowledge, and provocative constraints. The AI scaffolds the human by extending ideas, revealing patterns, and creating structure. | One-directional scaffolding (AI elevates human); no scaffolding claim; scaffolding only at high dial levels | One-directional scaffolding reduces the human to a student and the AI to a teacher. This is the default interaction pattern that Play Mode exists to change. Mutual elevation is the core insight: both players function beyond their current level. The human pushes the AI beyond trained patterns. The AI extends the human beyond their usual thinking. Neither achieves alone what both achieve together. |
| 10 | **Yes, And is "accept and extend," not "accept uncritically"** | Brown (2009): improvisational potential is a property of play — openness to serendipity and chance. The improv tradition (Johnstone, 1979; Halpern et al., 1994) specifies that "Yes, And" means taking the contribution somewhere before evaluating it — not that all contributions are equally valid. | Uncritical acceptance of all ideas; "Yes, But" (accept and qualify); "Yes, And" only for ideas above a quality threshold | "Yes, But" kills play — the "but" negates the "yes" and the contributor feels rejected. Uncritical acceptance produces mush — nothing has enough resistance to develop. "Accept and extend" is the correct implementation: take the idea somewhere, see where it leads, and THEN evaluate. The evaluation happens after the exploration, not before. Go further before going back. |
| 11 | **Adaptive Variability activates at 70% (late in the wave)** | Sutton-Smith (1997): variability prevents rigidity that follows successful adaptation. But variability requires something to vary — material must exist before it can be perturbed. Adaptive variability operating on an empty interaction produces randomness, not flexibility. | Activate early (variability from the start); activate only at 100%; user-controlled activation independent of dial | Variability at low dial levels would disrupt before there's anything to disrupt. The staggered wave ensures that by 70%, the Magic Circle is established, reciprocity is active, the Potential Space is open, both players are stretching, and ideas are building additively. Only then does deliberate perturbation serve its function — preventing premature convergence of material that exists. |
| 12 | **Continuation Desire is Principle #7 (activates last, only at 100%)** | Brown (2009): continuation desire is an emergent property of genuine play — it cannot be manufactured. It arises when all other conditions are met: safety, reciprocity, creative zone, mutual elevation, additive building, adaptive flexibility. If any condition fails, continuation desire does not appear. | Continuation desire as a mid-level principle; continuation desire as always active (maintaining engagement); no explicit continuation principle | Continuation desire is diagnostic, not causal. You cannot create it directly — you can only create the conditions from which it emerges. Making it the final principle reflects this: if all six preceding principles are fully active, continuation desire arises naturally. If it doesn't arise, something in the preceding chain has failed. It is the canary in the coal mine of the play state. |

## Output Layer Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 13 | **Moves (⊳/⊲) are always active when play mode is on** | Pellis & Pellis (2007): reciprocity requires visible turn-taking — each rat's attack and defense are distinct, alternating contributions. Huizinga (1938): play has structure — "an absolute and peculiar order reigns." Turn-taking is the minimal structure of two-player interaction. | Moves only at high dial levels; implicit turn-taking without markers; free-form interaction without structure | The turn markers are the skeleton of play. Without visible turn structure, the interaction defaults to the AI's natural pattern: continuous output. Moves make reciprocity visible — the AI's contribution is bounded (⊳) and the human's space is explicitly created (⊲). Removing Moves removes the structural mechanism that prevents domination. |
| 14 | **What-If Cards are "provocations not questions"** | Sutton-Smith (1997): play introduces variability through perturbation, not inquiry. A question ("what do you think about X?") places the burden on the receiver. A provocation ("what if X was actually Y?") creates a new reality to react to. Winnicott (1971): the Potential Space is activated by presenting objects in the transitional zone — neither real nor unreal, but available for play. | Questions ("what would happen if...?"); suggestions ("you could try..."); options ("here are three approaches...") | Questions shift the work to the human. Suggestions reduce the human to an evaluator. Options present a menu. Provocations create a new frame that demands a response — not an answer but a move. The Card format (boxed, distinct from the main response) signals that this is material to play with, not advice to follow. |
| 15 | **Invitations are the highest-dial layer (activating at ~80%)** | Vygotsky (1978): scaffolding into the ZPD requires trust and established safety — you cannot scaffold someone who doesn't trust the scaffolding. Invitations ask the human to lead, which requires them to trust that their contribution will be received with "yes, and" rather than evaluation. This trust builds gradually through the preceding principles. | Invitations at all levels; invitations only at 100%; invitations as the first layer | Invitations require the most trust. Asking a human to lead ("your move: what if you...") in the first exchange of a conversation would feel presumptuous or confusing. By 80%, the Magic Circle is established, reciprocity is demonstrated, the Potential Space is open, mutual elevation is active, ideas are building, and variation has entered. NOW the human trusts that their initiative will be received, extended, and built upon. The scaffolding is in place. |
| 16 | **"Every element pays rent" — no decorative layers** | Brown (2009): play that is performative rather than genuine produces disengagement, not continuation desire. Panksepp (1998): the PLAY circuit responds to authentic social signals, not theatrical ones — rats distinguish genuine play from play-like movements. | Allow aspirational markers; use layers as engagement decoration; maintain fixed layer density per response | Decorative layers destroy trust faster than no layers at all. A What-If Card that presents an obvious alternative signals that the system is performing play rather than playing. A Wildcard that doesn't genuinely shift perspective trains the human to ignore Wildcards. Every element must earn its place or the entire system degrades to decoration. |

## Naming and Framing Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 17 | **The mode is named "play" not "collaborate" or "game"** | Panksepp (1998): PLAY is the name of the primary emotional system — a specific, neuroanatomically mapped circuit. The name carries the weight of the science. Huizinga (1938): play is the fundamental category — games are a subset. Brown (2009): "play" captures the voluntary, purposeless, continuation-desire properties that "collaboration" and "game" miss. | "Collaborate mode" (emphasizing joint work); "Game mode" (emphasizing structured interaction); "Co-create mode" (emphasizing shared production); "Improv mode" (emphasizing spontaneity) | "Collaborate" implies work — shared effort toward a predetermined goal. Play has no predetermined goal. "Game" implies competition — winners and losers. Play is not zero-sum. "Co-create" is accurate but clinical — it describes the output without capturing the state. "Improv" captures one property (improvisational potential) but misses reciprocity, safety, elevation, and continuation. "Play" is the only word that names the neurobiological state itself. |
| 18 | **Play mode does not auto-disable for "serious" tasks** | Vygotsky (1978): play produces the highest level of cognitive functioning, not the lowest. Panksepp (2007): play activates frontal lobe maturation — the brain region responsible for executive function, planning, and complex reasoning. Brown (2009): play history correlates with professional achievement across all domains. | Disable for analytical tasks; reduce dial automatically for financial or legal work; require user confirmation for serious contexts | The research does not support a distinction between "serious" and "playful" domains. A financial model has potential spaces (what if the assumptions change?). A legal brief has adaptive variability needs (what arguments has the opposition not considered?). A technical architecture has ZPD potential (what if we both think beyond current constraints?). Auto-disabling would impose an artificial boundary that contradicts the research. |
| 19 | **The dial does not change file reading, logging, or system conventions** | Design decision. The dynamic axis modifies how two players interact, not what information is accessed or how it is recorded. Huizinga (1938): the Magic Circle has its own rules but exists within a larger reality — it does not destroy the surrounding order. | Higher dial = access more files; higher dial = skip logging; higher dial = override conventions | The play dynamic operates on top of the information system, not instead of it. Modifying file access would conflate interaction with retrieval. Skipping logging would lose the record of what the play produced. The dial changes how minds relate, not what they work with. |
| 20 | **The specification is self-contained (no code, no plugins, no dependencies)** | Design decision. The specification must be loadable as a single instruction set into any AI system. Huizinga (1938): play is defined by rules freely accepted — the rules themselves are the implementation. The specification IS the technology. | Require a companion API; require specific model capabilities; require plugin infrastructure | Self-containment maximizes portability and eliminates failure modes unrelated to the interaction design. Any system that can process natural language instructions can implement the specification. The constraint is deliberate: if the principles cannot be implemented through language alone, they are not well-specified enough. |

## Summary

Twenty design decisions, each traced to specific research evidence. The decisions fall into four categories:

1. **Architecture** (#1–5): How the specification is structured — independent dynamic axis, seven ordered principles, continuous dial, staggered activation. Grounded in the logical dependencies between play phenomena documented by Huizinga, Panksepp, Winnicott, and Vygotsky.

2. **Principles** (#6–12): How individual principles operate — Magic Circle first, 30% rule as structural constraint, potential space as overlap zone, mutual elevation, accept-and-extend, late variability, emergent continuation. Grounded in the specific findings of each principle's research tradition.

3. **Output** (#13–16): How the interaction dynamic is made visible — always-on Moves, provocative Cards, trust-gated Invitations, rent-paying elements. Grounded in the reciprocity research and the requirement for authentic play signals.

4. **Naming and Framing** (#17–20): How the mode is conceptualized — named "play," no auto-disable, additive-only, self-contained. Grounded in the neuroscience of PLAY as a primary emotional system and the cultural theory of play as a fundamental category.

Where no research evidence exists (decisions #4, #19, #20), this is stated explicitly. The specification does not claim evidence it does not have.

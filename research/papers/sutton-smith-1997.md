# The Ambiguity of Play

**Author:** Brian Sutton-Smith, Graduate School of Education, University of Pennsylvania
**Published:** Harvard University Press, 1997
**ISBN:** 978-0674005815
**Field:** Play Theory, Developmental Psychology, Cultural Studies

---

## Abstract

*The Ambiguity of Play* is the most comprehensive theoretical treatment of play in the academic literature. Sutton-Smith addresses a persistent puzzle: why has every attempt to define play produced ambiguity? He identifies seven "rhetorics" (theoretical frameworks) that have been applied to play — progress, fate, power, identity, imagination, self, and frivolity — and demonstrates that each captures something real while missing other dimensions. His resolution: the ambiguity is not a failure of analysis. It reflects play's actual nature. Play's fundamental function is **adaptive variability** — the generation of novel behavioral and cognitive responses that prevent the rigidity produced by successful adaptation. This variability is inherently ambiguous because its value lies precisely in its unpredictability.

## Key Findings

- **Seven rhetorics of play.** (1) Progress — play as child development (Piaget, Vygotsky). (2) Fate — play as chance and destiny (gambling, divination). (3) Power — play as contest and domination (sports, competition). (4) Identity — play as community formation (festivals, rituals). (5) Imagination — play as fantasy and creativity (make-believe, art). (6) Self — play as individual peak experience (flow, solitary play). (7) Frivolity — play as nonsense, inversion, carnival. Each rhetoric captures a genuine dimension; none captures the whole.
- **Adaptive variability.** The core thesis: "Play provides a model of variability that allows for natural selection of ideas. As a form of mental feedback, play nullifies the rigidity that sets in after successful adaptation." Successful adaptation produces efficiency (the organism learns what works). Efficiency produces rigidity (the organism repeats what works). Rigidity produces vulnerability (the organism cannot respond to novel challenges). Play counters this by generating variation for its own sake.
- **The evolutionary logic.** Organisms that play maintain a reservoir of unexplored behavioral options. When the environment changes — when the successful strategy no longer works — playing organisms have alternatives available. Non-playing organisms are locked into patterns that were once adaptive but are now maladaptive. Play is the evolutionary insurance policy against a changing world.
- **Variability is the function, not the byproduct.** Earlier theories treated play's variability as noise — meaningless variation around a developmental signal. Sutton-Smith reverses this: the variability IS the signal. The function of play is to be variable, to resist convergence, to maintain the capacity for novelty.
- **The ambiguity is functional.** If play's purpose is to generate variability, then any single definition of play will necessarily be incomplete — because the phenomenon is designed to resist categorical containment. The ambiguity is not a bug. It is a feature.

## Methodology

Theoretical synthesis drawing on over 400 works in the play literature across multiple disciplines: developmental psychology, anthropology, evolutionary biology, cultural studies, game theory, literary criticism, and philosophy. Sutton-Smith's method is rhetorical analysis — examining how different fields conceptualize play, identifying the assumptions and emphases of each framework, and demonstrating both their insights and their blind spots. The book is theoretical rather than empirical, but it engages critically with empirical research from multiple traditions.

## Limitations

The adaptive variability thesis, while theoretically compelling, has not been directly tested experimentally. It is difficult to design an experiment that would confirm or falsify the claim that play's function is to maintain variability (rather than, say, skill practice, social bonding, or cognitive development). Sutton-Smith's rhetorical analysis, while comprehensive, is interpretive — another theorist could organize the same literature differently. The seven rhetorics may not be exhaustive. The book's emphasis on ambiguity, while intellectually honest, has been critiqued for making play theory resistant to empirical progress — if the phenomenon is defined as ambiguous, how do you make falsifiable predictions?

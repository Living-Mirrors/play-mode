# Playing and Reality

**Author:** D.W. Winnicott, Paddington Green Children's Hospital, London
**Published:** Tavistock Publications, 1971
**ISBN:** 978-0415345460
**Field:** Psychoanalysis, Developmental Psychology, Creativity Research

---

## Abstract

*Playing and Reality* presents Winnicott's mature theory of the relationship between play, creativity, and psychological health. The central concept is the "Potential Space" — the transitional zone between inner psychic reality and shared external reality where creative experience occurs. This space originates in the infant's relationship with transitional objects (the teddy bear, the blanket — objects that are both "me" and "not me") and persists throughout life as the zone in which cultural experience, creativity, and play take place. Winnicott argues that the capacity to inhabit this intermediate zone — neither collapsing into pure subjectivity nor contracting into pure objectivity — is the definition of creative living and psychological health.

## Key Findings

- **The Potential Space.** A transitional zone between inner reality (pure fantasy, subjective omnipotence) and external reality (objective facts, shared constraints). This space is neither purely subjective nor purely objective — it partakes of both. It is the zone where creative experience occurs, where play happens, and where cultural life takes place.
- **Transitional objects and phenomena.** The infant's teddy bear or blanket is the first occupant of the potential space. The object is paradoxical: it is both created by the infant (subjectively conceived) and found in the world (objectively perceived). This paradox is not to be resolved — it is the productive condition for creativity throughout life.
- **Play and creativity are the same phenomenon.** "It is in playing and only in playing that the individual child or adult is able to be creative and to use the whole personality, and it is only in being creative that the individual discovers the self." Play is not preparation for creativity. Play IS creativity in its most fundamental form.
- **The holding environment.** The potential space requires a "holding environment" — a context of safety and reliability — to function. When the environment is unreliable, the individual contracts into either pure subjectivity (fantasy, withdrawal) or pure objectivity (compliance, false self). The potential space collapses.
- **Psychotherapy as play.** "Psychotherapy takes place in the overlap of two areas of playing, that of the patient and that of the therapist. Psychotherapy has to do with two people playing together." When either party cannot play, the therapeutic task is to enable play before any content-level work begins.

## Methodology

Psychoanalytic clinical observation. Winnicott draws on decades of clinical work with children and adults at Paddington Green Children's Hospital, including the "Spatula Game" (observing infants' interactions with a spatula placed within reach as a transitional object), the "Squiggle Game" (a collaborative drawing game used in therapeutic consultations with children), and extended psychoanalytic treatments. The methodology is clinical and interpretive, not experimental.

## Key Quotes

> "The potential space between baby and mother, between child and family, between individual and society or the world, depends on experience which leads to trust. It can be looked upon as sacred to the individual in that it is here that the individual experiences creative living."

> "The place where cultural experience is located is in the potential space between the individual and the environment. The same can be said of playing. Cultural experience begins with creative living first manifested as play."

## Limitations

Winnicott's work is psychoanalytic — rooted in clinical observation and interpretation rather than controlled experiment. The concepts are rich but difficult to operationalize for empirical testing. The Potential Space has been critiqued as an unfalsifiable concept — how do you measure the "overlap between inner and outer reality"? Critics from the cognitive science tradition have noted that Winnicott provides a phenomenological description of creative experience without specifying the cognitive mechanisms involved. The cultural context (mid-20th century British psychoanalysis) may not translate universally, particularly regarding assumptions about mother-infant relationships and family structures.
